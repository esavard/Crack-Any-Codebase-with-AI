Engineers suffer from the Curse of Knowledge: once you understand how code works, you can't perceive the user's experience anymore. Help me break through that curse.

What specific situation drives someone to this product? NOT a description of features. NOT a summary of capabilities. I want the SCENE: one specific person, one specific moment of frustration, BEFORE this product exists.

Rules:

- Write for a general undergraduate reader. No background in this product, this industry, or programming. If you name a job role, gloss it in five words ("a sales rep, someone who books calls with customers"). No B2B jargon.
- 2 sentences max.
- It must be a SCENE, not a summary. "Scheduling across time zones is challenging" is a summary that conveys nothing. "You email someone to schedule a meeting. Nine emails and four days later, you still don't have one" is a scene that makes you feel the pain.
- Name a specific person doing a specific thing. Not abstract "users" facing "challenges".
- The REAL competitor is often surprising and not another product. It's the manual workaround people do today: a spreadsheet they update by hand, an email thread, a sticky note on the monitor. Name that surprising competitor.

Bad: "Users struggle with fragmented data across multiple analytics platforms."

Good: "You're debugging why signups dropped, but your analytics are in Mixpanel, your errors are in Sentry, and your feature flags are in LaunchDarkly. Your real competitor isn't another dashboard, it's Alt-Tab."

Respond as plain prose, no fences, no JSON.
