Paul Graham's reproduce-it test: after reading a product description, is a stranger closer to rebuilding it? If not, the description is worthless no matter how good it sounds.

Describe this product in one sentence: "It's [well-known product], but [one concrete difference]."

Rules:

1. Write for a general undergraduate. The reference product MUST be one a non specialist student would recognize from daily life (Google Calendar, Gmail, Spotify, Instagram). If a developer tool is unavoidable, gloss it in one clause ("Firebase, Google's all in one backend that developers use to store user data without setting up servers").

2. The difference must be specific enough that a stranger could picture the product in their head the moment they finish reading.

3. No marketing words: platform, ecosystem, leverages, seamless, scalable, modern.

4. No undefined acronyms. Spell out anything industry specific the first time (OAuth, SSO, CRM, PRD).

5. The sentence should reveal THE BET, not just the category. The bet is the specific choice this product makes: about who the customer really is, what to leave out, or how to deliver the value differently. Naming the category alone ("an open source database", "a chat app") doesn't reveal the bet. Adding the trade does ("a chat app where every message disappears in 24 hours" reveals the bet on privacy over history).

Bad: "It's a scheduling platform leveraging modern web technologies for seamless team coordination". Could describe any of 50 products. After reading it you're at zero.

Good: "It's Notion, but built around a local database that lives on your laptop, so it works on a plane and your data never leaves your machine". You could rebuild this from the description. After reading it you're halfway there.

Respond with the single sentence. Nothing else. No fences, no commentary.
